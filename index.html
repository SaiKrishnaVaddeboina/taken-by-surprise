Taken by Surprise: Mapping U.S. Cargo Theft in 2024

A Bayesian-Surprise & choropleth companion to the FBI Crime Data Explorer’s 2024 Cargo Theft release. We re-ask the question the raw map obscures — which states are unusual given their population — following Correll & Heer (2017) and Ndlovu, Shrestha & Harrison (2023).

Why we built this
A raw map rewards population, not risk.

Kentucky alone explains 31% of stolen value in the 2024 release — the kind of signal that dominates every other state on an undifferentiated dollar choropleth. Switch to the rate map and a second story lands: Maryland is 14× the national incident rate. The Bayesian surprise map reconciles both by asking whether each state’s rate is plausible under a shared sampling model.

Methodology
de Moivre funnel · signed square-root transform

We compute each state’s surprise as a z-score of its per-capita cargo-theft incident rate under a de Moivre sampling model centered on the national rate, following Correll & Heer (2017) and Ndlovu et al. (2023):

p̂ = (Σ incidents) / (Σ population)
Zi = (ratei − p̂) / √( p̂ · (1 − p̂) / popi )
surprisei = 2 · ( 1 − Φ(|Zi|) )

Because cargo-theft incidents are rare (p̂ ≈ 7.7 per 100k), classical p-values saturate at zero for most states. We therefore encode the signed square-root of z — sign(z)·√|z| — on the map, so that both the magnitude and direction of the deviation are legible. Value-per-capita surprise is computed analogously as a Gaussian z-score across states.
